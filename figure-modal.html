Quick Facts
  • ATP binding causes detachment
  • Pi (phosphate) triggers the power stroke
  • The cycle repeats as long as ATP is available
Common Misconception
“The release of ADP causes the power stroke.”
WRONG! ADP does NOT cause the power stroke.
The release of the phosphate ion (Pi) triggers the myosin head to snap forward causing the actin filament to be pulled inwards (power stroke).
What is this?
This is a diagram of an actin-myosin cross-bridge cycle, which is used by muscle cells to undergo muscle contraction.
How do I understand it?
  1. Step 1 - Myosin is attached to the actin filament.
  2. Step 2 - ATP binds to the myosin head, releasing it from the actin filament.
  3. Step 3 - ATP is hydrolyzed to produce one ADP and one phosphate ion (Pi). The energy released by hydrolysis causes the myosin head to cock back.
  4. Step 4 - Myosin head attaches itself to the actin filament again while still holding ADP and Pi.
  5. Step 5 - ADP leaves following Pi, causing the myosin head to pivot forward and pull the actin filament inwards (this is the power stroke!).
  6. Step 6 - Once ADP leaves, the myosin head is attached back to the actin filament to prepare for the next cycle.
